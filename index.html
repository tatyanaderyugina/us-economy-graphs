US Economy Barometer

How the US economy is doing, illustrated with simple graphs.

Big economic numbers are easy to argue about and hard to picture. This page tracks the essentials — jobs, prices, housing, paychecks, and how Americans feel about it all — using official government and university data. Every graph starts in January 2024 and is updated monthly as new numbers come out.

Jobs

Is it easy to find work? The job market is the foundation of most families' finances.

Unemployment rate

The share of people who want to work but don't have a job. Lower is better.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics. Data series is adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Jobs added each month

How many jobs the economy gained or lost versus the month before, in thousands. A healthy economy adds jobs most months.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics payroll survey. Monthly change in total nonfarm employment, adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Prices

How fast is the cost of living rising? Index charts show the change since January 2024 (start = 100), and the dollar charts show what things actually cost.

Consumer Price Index: All

Overall consumer prices. A value of 107 means prices are 7% higher than in January 2024.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics. Data series is adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Consumer Price Index: Medical care

The same measure, for medical care — doctor visits, hospital services, prescription drugs, and health insurance.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics. Data series is adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Grocery prices

Average US prices of everyday staples: ground beef, milk, eggs, and bread.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics average price data. U.S. city average prices, not adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Gas price

The average pump price of a gallon of regular unleaded gasoline.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics average price data. U.S. city average price, not adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Housing

For most families, housing is the single biggest expense.

Consumer Price Index: Housing

The cost of shelter and utilities — rent, homeowners' costs, and household energy — relative to January 2024.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics. Data series is adjusted for seasonality. Link to data page.

Mortgage rate

The average rate on a new 30-year fixed mortgage. Lower rates make buying a home more affordable.

Source: Freddie Mac Primary Mortgage Market Survey. Weekly 30-year fixed rates averaged by month. Link to data page.

The national debt

What the federal government owes, and what it costs to carry it.

Federal debt

Total debt of the US federal government, in trillions of dollars. It grows when Washington spends more than it collects.

Source: U.S. Treasury, Debt to the Penny. Total public debt outstanding, end of each month. Link to data page.

Interest on the debt

What the government paid in interest on its debt over the previous 12 months. More debt and higher interest rates both push this up — money that cannot go to anything else.

Source: U.S. Treasury, Interest Expense on the Public Debt Outstanding. Rolling 12-month total. Link to data page.

Paychecks and the public mood

Are wages keeping up with prices — and how do Americans feel about the economy?

Inflation-adjusted pay

Hourly pay of typical private-sector workers (excluding managers), with inflation removed. When this line rises, pay is growing faster than prices.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics. Average hourly earnings of production and nonsupervisory employees (about four-fifths of private workers), adjusted for inflation using the Consumer Price Index and expressed in the latest month's dollars. Link to data page.

Wages across the income distribution

Inflation-adjusted weekly pay of full-time workers near the bottom (10th percentile), middle (median), and top (90th percentile) of the pay scale, relative to early 2024. Shows whose pay is keeping up.

Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics, usual weekly earnings of full-time wage and salary workers (Current Population Survey). Quarterly data, adjusted for inflation using the Consumer Price Index. Link to data page.

Consumer confidence

How optimistic households feel about their finances and the economy, by income group. Higher means more optimistic.

Source: University of Michigan Survey of Consumers. Data series: Index of Consumer Sentiment Within Income Terciles. Link to data page.

Net sentiment about future business conditions

The share of people expecting good business conditions a year from now, minus the share expecting bad ones. Below zero, pessimists outnumber optimists.

Source: University of Michigan Survey of Consumers. Data series: Expected Change in Business Conditions in a Year. Net economic sentiment is calculated as the difference between percent with a favorable outlook and percent with an unfavorable outlook. Link to data page.
Data: Bureau of Labor Statistics, University of Michigan Survey of Consumers, Freddie Mac, U.S. Treasury. Updated July 2026
